    The dataset used in this work was from the Ontario Open Data Catalogue: Rabies Cases in Ontario. I chose this because I am a microbiologist and I am very interested in tracking zoonotic diseases such as Rabies.

    Link to the data:
    https://data.ontario.ca/dataset/rabies-cases-in-ontario
    
    
    > What software did you use to create your data visualization?
    I created this visualization using Microsoft Excel, specifically to organize the data, generate the bar chart, add axis labels and data labels, and export the final figure as a .png image.

    > Who is your intended audience? 
    As with the Visualization 1, for Visualization 2, the intended audience includes public health professionals, wildlife disease researchers, microbiologists, and members of the public that are interested in zoonotic diseases such as Rabies.
    
    > What information or message are you trying to convey with your visualization? 
    The visualization illustrates the number of animals tested for rabies in Ontario between 2015-2025. The message I am trying to convey here is how surveillance effort has changed over time and provide more context to interpret trends seen in confirmed rabies cases. The figure demonstrates that surveillance activity remained relatively high throughout the study period despite the year-to-year fluctuations. Importantly, surveillance activity was substantially low in the year 2015. 
    
    > What aspects of design did you consider when making your visualization? How did you apply them? With what elements of your plots? 
    I selected a bar chart as it allows for the straightforward comparison of yearly testing totals. I displayed the bars in a single dark green colour to avoud unnecessary visual complexity and keep attention focused on the data. I added clear axis labels and a descriptive title. Additionally, I included data labels above each bar so viewers can quickly identify exact testing values without the need to estimate. I did maintain grid lines to allow for comparisons across the years while maintaining the readability.
    
    > How did you ensure that your data visualizations are reproducible? If the tool you used to make your data visualization is not reproducible, how will this impact your data visualization? 
    While Microsoft Excel does not automatically preserve every step of the workflow in the same way as I did for visualization 1, the source data and chart settings can be shared so that others can reproduce my visualization 2. 
    
    > How did you ensure that your data visualization is accessible?  
    To ensure accessibility, I used a simple chart design with high contrast between the bars and background. Importantly, I do not rely on colour alone here to communicate information. In fact, I provide the exact values through data labels. I also generated clear axis labels, title, and readable font sizes to improve comprehension by the viewers.
    
    > Who are the individuals and communities who might be impacted by your visualization? 
    Public health agencies, wildlife disease surveillance programs, vets, researchers, policy makers, and interested public. The information presented in visualization 2 may influence the effors and resources dedicated to monitoring rabies within Ontario.
    
    > How did you choose which features of your chosen dataset to include or exclude from your visualization? 
    I focused on testing volume alone here as this chart is meant to complement the first figure and provide additional context for interpreting trends in rabies incidence.
