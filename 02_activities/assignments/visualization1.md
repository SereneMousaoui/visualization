    The dataset used in this work was from the Ontario Open Data Catalogue: Rabies Cases in Ontario. I chose this because I am a microbiologist and I am very interested in tracking zoonotic diseases such as Rabies.

    Link to the data:
    https://data.ontario.ca/dataset/rabies-cases-in-ontario
    
    > What software did you use to create your data visualization?
    For visualization 1, I used Python - particularly employing Pandas, Seaborn and Matplotlib.

    > Who is your intended audience? 
    The intended audience is public health professionals, wildlife disease researchers, microbiologists, and members of the public that are interested in zoonotic diseases such as Rabies.
    
    > What information or message are you trying to convey with your visualization? 
    This visualization illustrates the trends in laboratory confirmed rabies cases in Ontario from 2015-2025. It clearly highlights the dramatic spike in raccoon-variant rabies in 2016 followed by a substantial decline in subsequent years. Clearly, some efforts have been employed to bring the spike under control and this visualization demonstrates that control in the raccoon-variant rabies while the fox-variant rabies remained relatively uncommon during the span of the study period.
    
    > What aspects of design did you consider when making your visualization? How did you apply them? With what elements of your plots? 
    I selected the line-chart because the primary goal was to communicate changes over time. I used different colour and marker shapes to distinguish the rabies variants. I also have clear axis labels and descriptive title and a legend to improve interpretability. Additionally, I included gridlines to facilitate the viewer's ability to compare between years while maintaining the readability of the visualization. I kept the layout simple to minimize unnecessary visual clutter and direct attention to the trends in the data.
    
    > How did you ensure that your data visualizations are reproducible? If the tool you used to make your data visualization is not reproducible, how will this impact your data visualization? 
    I created this visualization with Python entirely using documented code. The dataset is publicly available through the Ontartio Open Data Catalogue, allowing others to reproduce the analysis and figure using the same script.
    
    > How did you ensure that your data visualization is accessible?  
    I considered accessibility by using colorblind-friendly Seaborn palette, which ensures that the twp data series remain distinguishable for viewers with colour-vision issues. I also generated clear labels,  a readable font size, and markers in addition to colour which allows the viewers to differentiate lines even if the colours are difficult to perceive.
    
    > Who are the individuals and communities who might be impacted by your visualization?  
    This visualization may impact a wide range of individuals and communities beyond those who work directly with rabies curveillance. Public health officials and policy makers may use information about disease trends to inform surveillence programs and prevention strategies. More broadly, Ontario residents may be affected by how rabies risk is communicated, especially those who live in areas where wildlife-human interactions are common. Critically, pet owners may also be impacted, as information about rabies trends can influence decisions regarding vaccination and animal safety. Finally, researchers (like myself) may use these data to better understand zoonotic disease dynamics.
    
    > How did you choose which features of your chosen dataset to include or exclude from your visualization? 
    I chose to present yearly counts of laboratory-confirmed raccoon and fox variant rabies cases because they directly support the goal of examining temporal disease trends. The pther variables, such as the number of animals tested, were excluded from this visualization because they address surveillance effort rather then disease occurence (and I explored this aspect in the second visualization).
    
    > What ‘underwater labour’ contributed to your final data visualization product?
    This visualization is only the visible outcome of a much larger process of decision making and preparation - much of which remains hidden from the audience. This includes selecting the approporiate dataset, learning about rabies surveillance in Ontario, and determining which variables were most relevant to the story being told and how best to communicate it. I think more broadly, there is a much larger network of labour and decision making. This includes the work of wildlife surveillance staff, vets, lab technicians, field personnel, data managers, and government agencies who collected, tested, verified and published the data in this project. Without this, the dataset wouldn't exist.
